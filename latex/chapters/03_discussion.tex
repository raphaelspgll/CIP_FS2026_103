\section{Discussion}

\subsection{RQ1 — Which Indicators Relate Most Strongly to Price Changes?}

To answer the first research question, lag-1 Pearson correlations were computed between each
stationary feature at day $t-1$ and \texttt{price\_direction} at day $t$.
Non-stationary level features (\texttt{log\_close}, \texttt{ma\_7}, \texttt{ma\_30},
\texttt{log\_market\_cap}) were excluded beforehand. They trend with the price level and exhibit
pairwise correlations of $r \geq 0.91$, which would produce spurious associations with the target.

The strongest statistically significant predictor for Bitcoin and XRP is \texttt{log\_return}
($r = -0.066$, $p = 0.023$ and $r = -0.081$, $p = 0.006$ respectively), indicating a weak
mean-reversion tendency. A positive return yesterday is slightly more likely to be followed by
a downward move today.
For ICP the significant predictor is \texttt{volatility\_7} ($r = -0.062$, $p = 0.034$),
suggesting that elevated recent volatility weakly predicts a downward direction.
All $r^2$ values remain below $0.7\,\%$, confirming that the relationships are statistically
detectable but practically negligible.

\subsection{RQ2 — How Accurately Can Indicators Predict Price Direction?}

\textbf{ACF analysis sets the predictability ceiling.}
The autocorrelation function (ACF) measures whether past returns linearly predict future returns.
Under the weak-form Efficient Market Hypothesis, all ACF coefficients would be zero.
Figure~\ref{fig:acf} shows the ACF for lags 1 to 20 for all three coins, with 95\,\% confidence
bands of $\pm 1.96 / \sqrt{n} = \pm 0.057$.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.31\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../images/autocorr_bitcoin.png}
  \end{minipage}\hfill
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.31\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../images/autocorr_xrp.png}
  \end{minipage}\hfill
  \begin{minipage}[t]{0.31\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../images/autocorr_icp.png}
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{ACF of daily log returns for Bitcoin (left), XRP (centre), and ICP (right), lags 1 to 20.
           Dashed lines mark the 95\,\% confidence band ($\pm 0.057$). Nearly all coefficients fall
           within the band, confirming near-random walk behaviour. XRP shows a slightly negative
           lag-1 coefficient, consistent with the mean-reversion signal in Figure~\ref{fig:lag1}.}
  \label{fig:acf}
\end{figure}

Nearly all coefficients fall within the confidence band across all three coins, confirming
near-random walk behaviour.
The accuracy ceiling of 52 to 58\,\% is derived from three observations.
First, the class balance sets the trivial floor. A classifier that always predicts the majority
class achieves 50.4\,\% (BTC), 51.1\,\% (XRP), and 52.6\,\% (ICP) without learning anything.
Second, the ACF confirms the series is statistically consistent with white noise, so the realistic
accuracy is very close to this floor.
Third, the significant lag-1 correlations (max $|r| = 0.081$ for XRP) provide a rough upper bound.
For a binary target with a single predictor of correlation $r$ and class probability $P \approx 0.5$,
the maximum accuracy gain from a linear classifier is approximately
$|r| \cdot \sqrt{P(1-P)} \approx 0.081 \times 0.5 \approx 4$ percentage points above the baseline,
placing the theoretical ceiling at roughly 54 to 58\,\%.
Any result substantially above this range would indicate data leakage, which was confirmed when
initial experiments with unlagged features produced 100\,\% Decision Tree accuracy.

\textbf{Rolling volatility and market regimes.}
Figure~\ref{fig:volatility} shows the 30-day rolling standard deviation of log returns for all
three coins.
Volatility is not constant over time. Distinct high-volatility regimes are visible, particularly
in 2022 and early 2024.
ICP consistently shows the highest volatility (mean $\sigma_7 = 4.41\,\%$), followed by XRP
(3.41\,\%) and Bitcoin (2.23\,\%).
This time-varying structure suggests that a static model trained on the full period may not capture
regime-specific dynamics, which is a further reason why accuracy is limited.

\textbf{RQ2.}
All accuracy values lie between 40 and 63\,\%, consistent with the ceiling established by the ACF
analysis. This confirms that yesterday's market indicators are insufficient for reliable
next-day price direction prediction.

\textbf{RQ3.}
Neither model consistently outperforms the other across all three coins.
Logistic Regression is marginally more stable.
The ICP accuracy of 62.6\,\% for Logistic Regression appears high but is undermined by a recall of
only 14.1\,\%, revealing that the model almost exclusively predicts the majority class rather than
learning a genuine signal.
The Decision Tree gains no benefit from its higher capacity, confirming that the data lacks the
non-linear structure required to justify a more complex model.
